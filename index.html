About Me

I'm Thomas Hersbach, a 4th year PhD Candidate in the Catalysis and Surface Chemistry (CASC) group at Leiden University.

I obtained my BSc in Molecular Science & Technology with distinction in 2012, from Leiden University and Delft University of Technology (co-op bachelors program). My thesis for this degree was conducted in the CASC group and explored the promoting effect of various adatoms on selective electro-oxidation of glycerol on platinum. This work was later published in the Topics in Catalysis.

In 2012 I began my masters at Leiden University. During this MSc progam, I followed the extracurricular Leiden Leadership Programme. As part of the regular curriculum, I conducted two main research projects that were completed with theses, which led to publications in JACS and JPCL. The work of my first thesis was conducted in the CASC group and focused on the spectro-electrochemical characterization of an iridium-based oxygen evolution catalyst. The second thesis was written during my final master's semester. This semester was spent at Stanford University, where I worked in the Jaramillo group to investigate the promoting effect of noble metals on the oxygen evolution activity of 3d metal oxides. Following the completion of this project, I received my MSc degree with distinction in August 2014.

After obtaining my MSC degree, I started my PhD project under supervision of physical electrochemist Marc Koper. The project focuses on cathodic corrosion: a counter-intuitive electrochemical process in which electrodes corrode under cathodic (negative) polarization. The corrosion process induces remarkable modifications in the corroded electrode and is able to generate nanoparticles as a corrosion product. My studies of cathodic corrosion have currently led to two publications in which the onset of cathodic corrosion is explored on platinum, rhodium and gold. A third publication revolves around alloy nanoparticles that form by corroding PtRh alloys; these alloy particles do not always have the same elemental distribution as their parent alloys.
